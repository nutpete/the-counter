Everything you need to know about how we build AI tools for your teams.

We set up in your office for 2–4 days. Your team brings real problems — the things that slow down their actual work. We listen, understand the context, and build functional AI tools on the spot.

What we build falls into two tiers. Tier 1 tools are simple, quick wins — things we can build and hand over in hours. Tier 2 tools are more complex features that need development time beyond the engagement, but we scope them out and show you what the path looks like.

Some problems fall outside what AI tooling can solve. When that happens, we'll tell you — and where we can, we'll suggest strategic alternatives worth exploring.

The real outcome is momentum. When people see that bringing a problem results in a usable tool in days, they start thinking differently about what's possible.

ROI comes in a few forms:

  • Time savings: If a tool eliminates 4 hours of work per week per person, that's measurable and compounds fast.
  • Quality improvement: Better outputs, fewer errors, faster turnaround — especially in customer-facing or compliance-sensitive work.
  • Capability expansion: Your team can now do things they couldn't before.
  • Organizational learning: Your people learn how to think about AI in their actual work, not in theory.
  • Adaptive capacity: People know how to use freed-up time effectively. They have the knowledge and skills to define how their work evolves, not just follow a new process.

We recommend picking 2–3 metrics before we start and measuring them 30, 60, and 90 days out.

Either approach works. You can go deeper with a specific team — focus the engagement on one department and build tools tailored to their problems. Or you can go broader, open it up across the organization and let multiple teams bring problems at once.

The broader approach often creates more cross-pollination. Tools built for one team inspire others. The focused approach builds deeper capability in one area first, then expands.

Real-world use always reveals things we didn't anticipate. A tool might work perfectly for its original use case but then someone realizes it could work for something slightly different, with small tweaks.

During the engagement, we iterate fast. After we leave, your team gets a roadmap for minor improvements, and we're available if you want to refine things. For major evolution or new tools, that's where we discuss ongoing support or additional engagements.

The goal is tools that start strong and can grow with your needs, not tools that get locked into their original design.

The Counter is meant to be the first step. It proves that AI tools can solve real work problems. It builds confidence in your teams. It shows leadership what's possible.

From there, patterns emerge. You notice certain problem types come up repeatedly. You realize certain teams are hungry to build more. Your IT team understands how to support these tools in your environment.

That's when ongoing capability building happens — either through additional Counter engagements, your teams building their own tools with our guidance, or embedding AI-thinking into how your organization approaches work.

The Counter isn't a one-off. It's a catalyst for a bigger shift in how your organization thinks about AI and work.
